Saleem Raja Haja, a computer scientist with degrees in applied mathematics and computer science, needed a roller coaster physics simulator to help college students understand the underlying mechanics of what they ride. He requested a week. He received a red-teamed, citation-backed, zero-dependency live simulator in 24 hours. This is the record of how that happened — and what it demonstrates about the methodology.

// 01
The Brief
What WasRequested

Saleem and Sheldon connected via a 30-minute Zoom meeting — the first meeting. Saleem came in with a computer science and applied mathematics background and a research interest in AI governance and agentic systems. He wanted to understand the work Sheldon was doing on immutable ledger infrastructure using GitHub and blockchain technology.

By the end of the meeting, Saleem had a clear problem to hand off: he wanted a framework and working code for a roller coaster physics simulation that could help college students understand the physical forces at play — g-forces, energy conservation, velocity through different track configurations.

He agreed to send a PDF with the project requirements via LinkedIn immediately after the meeting. Sheldon asked for one week to develop the framework and code, explicitly noting he would work with AI assistance to build it. A follow-up meeting was scheduled for one week out.

The brief arrived as promised. The requirements were clear: physics-accurate, educational focus, something college students could interact with to build intuition about roller coaster dynamics. No specific technology requirement — Saleem mentioned Python initially, but the delivery format was left to the architect.

What Saleem expected to receive in a week: a framework document and some working code. What he actually received within 24 hours was an interactive, zero-dependency HTML simulator with citation-backed physics equations, real-time computation across multiple track sections, g-force and safety limit display, and the AION Verified badge after passing red team and arithmetic audit.

The one-week estimate was Sheldon's conservative margin, not a capacity constraint. The methodology compresses the timeline — the scoping conversation was the framework document, and the build followed the specification cleanly.

The Methodology in Operation
What ThisDemonstrates

The one-week estimate was not caution about capability. It was an honest margin from an architect who had not yet had the scoping conversation. The brief arrived clear. The failure modes were obvious from the domain — physics simulators used in educational contexts must not present dangerous configurations as safe, must not produce wrong outputs at edge inputs, and must not be so fragile that they break when a student enters an unrealistic value.

Once those failure modes were named, the framework document wrote quickly. Once the framework document was written, the build specification was complete. The AI-assisted implementation followed the specification. The verification passed because the specification had been tested before the build, not after it.

Saleem's background — applied mathematics and computer science — meant the scoping conversation was technically precise. He knew what physically accurate meant. He had a clear audience in mind. The brief was actionable from the first sentence. That is the fastest possible intake for this methodology.

The 24-hour delivery is not the benchmark for every engagement. It is the benchmark for an engagement with a clear domain, a technically literate client, and a brief that arrived with no ambiguity. When those three conditions hold, 24 hours is the real number — not a marketing claim, but a documented outcome with a timestamped delivery record and a verification log to match.

What this case study demonstrates about rapid prototyping
  • The framework document is the compression point. The clearer the scoping conversation, the faster everything that follows it.
  • AI-assisted development is the honest description of the build method. AION Verified means the output was red-teamed and audited — the badge makes no claim about how the code was written.
  • Zero dependencies was the right call for a student-facing educational tool. The platform decision belongs in the framework document, not in the brief.
  • The one-week estimate was not a capacity constraint — it was a margin held for unknown scope. When the scope was clear, the margin collapsed to 24 hours.
  • Verification added by default, not requested. The badge was not in Saleem's brief. It was in the delivery because AION Verified is the standard, not an option.
  • The follow-up meeting one week out became a review of a working product. That is what the methodology is designed to produce.
